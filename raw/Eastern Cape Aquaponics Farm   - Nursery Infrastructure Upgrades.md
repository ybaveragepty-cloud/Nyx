General RAS references describe at least **1 turnover/hour** as standard, while flow-through tilapia guidance gives a separate rule of **100% replacement every 90–120 minutes**. [RWFM Extension+1](https://extension.rwfm.tamu.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/8/2013/09/Fish-Farming-in-Recirculating-Aquaculture-Systems-RAS.pdf?utm_source=chatgpt.com)

At **1 turnover/hour**:

- **Per tank:** 2,500 L/h = **2.5 m³/h**

## 2) Heat pump size

This depends on **how the nursery is run**:

### Case A — Recirculating nursery

Here, the heat pump mainly covers:

- tank heat loss to air
- heat loss in filters/sump/piping
- makeup-water heating
- initial pull-up from cold start

In a recirculating nursery, the **recirculation flow does not by itself determine heat-pump size**. The required thermal capacity depends mostly on ambient temperature, insulation, exposed water area, and how quickly you want to recover temperature after water changes.

### Case B — Flow-through nursery

If you are continuously replacing water, heating demand becomes very large. Based on the tilapia flow-through rule of **100% replacement every 90–120 minutes**, a 20,000 L nursery would need about **10–13.3 m³/h** of new water, and heating that continuously is a very different load. [Aquaculture](https://aquaculture.mgcafe.uky.edu/sites/aquaculture.ca.uky.edu/files/srac_282_tank_culture_of_tilapia.pdf?utm_source=chatgpt.com)

For **8 nursery tanks totaling 20 m³**, a realistic installed heat-pump range is usually:

- **10–12 kW thermal** if tanks are indoors / insulated / low winter exposure
- **14–18 kW thermal** if in a shed/tunnel with moderate heat loss
- **20–30 kW thermal** if poorly insulated, exposed, or you want fast recovery after grading / water changes

For Eastern Cape-type winter conditions, if this nursery is not in a fully insulated room, I would not start below **15 kW thermal**, and **18–20 kW thermal** is the safer engineering selection.
