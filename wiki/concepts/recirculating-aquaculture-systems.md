# Recirculating Aquaculture Systems

RAS is the core technology for indoor, controlled-environment fish production. Water circulates through a closed loop: fish tanks, mechanical filtration (solids removal), biofiltration (ammonia conversion), aeration/oxygenation, temperature control, and back to tanks. The system reuses 90-99% of its water, making it viable in water-scarce regions like South Africa.

## System Components

A commercial RAS installation requires:

- **Fish tanks** — sized for target stocking density (e.g., 60 kg/m³ for tilapia grow-out)
- **Mechanical filtration** — removes solid waste (uneaten feed, feces) before it decomposes
- **Biofiltration** — nitrifying bacteria convert toxic ammonia (TAN) to nitrite (NOB), then nitrite to nitrate. This is the biological heart of the system
- **Aeration** — maintains dissolved oxygen at target levels (7-8+ mg/L for trout, 6-7 mg/L for tilapia)
- **Heating** — critical for tropical species like tilapia (target 28°C). The Eastern Cape facility required Alliance Smart Aqua R32 18kW inverter heat pumps and ITS 35kW inverters for grow-out
- **Pumps** — sized for required flow rates. The Eastern Cape nursery spec calls for 32-36 m³/hr across 20,000L

## The Nitrification Sequence

When a biofilter fails or is undersized, the failure signature is predictable: ammonia spikes first (AOB bacteria affected), then nitrite accumulates (NOB bacteria affected). Understanding this sequence is critical for diagnosing system stress — it tells the operator exactly what is failing and how urgently.

> Ammonia testing: Daily minimum in commercial systems (not weekly like hobby)

## Stocking and Production

The Eastern Cape facility targets 1,000 kg tilapia per month to market at 600-800g harvest weight with a 10-11 month grow-out cycle. This requires running [[staggered-cohort-production]] with multiple age groups simultaneously, which in turn demands that the RAS infrastructure be engineered with significant redundancy per the [[200-percent-engineering-doctrine]].

## RAS vs. Other Aquaculture

RAS is distinct from pond, raceway, and cage culture. Its advantages are water efficiency, environmental control, and biosecurity. Its disadvantages are capital intensity, energy costs (pumps and heating run 24/7), and technical complexity. The La Buschagne model accepts these costs because the control they provide is essential for consistent, franchise-grade production.
